Patterns Of Development

refers to the particular strategy used by a writer to develop ideas.

Types of Patterns in Development

Narration

- means to tell a story about people, places, events, dates, etc.

Elements of a Narrative Text
Setting

- tells about the time, place, atmosphere, and mood of the event.
For example:
"One cold morning at the Coffee shop. "
"I woke up happily in my room at 6 a.m.”

Characters

- pertains to a person involved in a story.

Types of Characters:

Protagonist or the Bida
Antagonist or the Kontrabida

Plot

- refers to the sequence of related events in a story.

Parts of Plot
Exposition

- refers to the beginning of the story where the characters and setting are introduced.

Rising Action

- it is when the conflict is exposed.

Climax

- the highest point of interest and the turning point of the story.

Falling Action

- It is when the conflict begins to be resolved.

Denouement

- refers to the final resolution of the story.



Point of View

- refers to the perspective of the writer in narrating a story.

Types of Point of View:
First-person POV

- the reader accesses the story through one person. - use of pronouns I, we, my, and our.
For example:
"I told my mother that we lost our passports.

Second-person POV

- is structured around the pronouns you, your, and yours.
< For example:
"You instruct the police to bring the prisoner to your office.

Third-person POV

- uses the pronouns he, his, she, hers, they, and their.
For example:
"She told him that their assessment of the situation was incorrect.”

Description

- gives information of what a person, an object, a place, or a situation is like.
- it mainly appeals to the readers' senses.
- uses signal words such as above, under, besides, big, tall, yellowish, and some other adjectives.
For example:
"Andy walks above the roof with a big knife stuck in his foot."

Definition

- explains what a concept, term, or subject means, does, what something is used for, and what something looks like.
- signal words for this pattern are is defined as, as defined as, refers to, means, to define, and to illustrate.
For example:
"Bawl means to cry. " "Plot refers to the series of events. " Exemplification - uses specific or concrete examples to expound the main idea. uses signal phrases like for example, for instance, namely, consider the following, etc. For example: "My dog, Oso, is learning new tricks. For example, he can sit, stay, and lie down. " "They bought snacks, namely sandwiches and soda.”

Classification

- sorts items, people, or objects to group together those with similar characteristics.
For example:
"the medicine as a profession: pediatrician, dermatologist, cardiologist."

Comparison and Contrast

Comparison means to identify the similarities of two different items.
Contrast means to identify the differences between two similar items.
- You cannot compare and contrast two items that do not share some common characteristics.

Cause and Effect

- means to identify the reasons and results of an occurrence.
For Example:
“I did not study last night. As a result, I failed the quiz.”
“I went to the school at 8 a.m.; therefore, I was late.”

Problem - Solving

- starts by identifying a problem and then proposes two or more solutions.
For Example:
“Deforestation has heightened global warming. To solve this problem, humans should stop cutting trees and plant more trees instead.”

Persuasion

- It intends to convince readers to believe in an idea or opinion and to do an action.
For Example:
“To lessen the cases of teenage pregnancy, teenagers should abstain from having sex or should use contraceptives.”

Organization

- also known as arrangement, refers to the chronological and logical arrangement of the ideas in a text.
For example:
Therefore, I am a human. All humans are mortal. I am mortal.
For example:
All humans are mortal. I am mortal. Therefore, I am a human.

3 Organizational Techniques
Physical Format

- is an aspect of the organization that is immediately seen or is apparent to the reader.
For example:
All humans are mortal. I am mortal. Therefore, I am a human.

Signal Words

- are textual cues that readers can use to follow a text.
For example:
All humans are mortal. I am mortal. Therefore, I am a human.

Structure

- provides the framework upon which the text is organized: the introduction, body, and conclusion.
For example:
All humans are mortal. I am mortal. Therefore, I am a human.

Coherence and Cohesion
Coherence

- refers to the overall sense of unity in a passage or text.

Cohesion

- refers to the connection of your ideas both at the sentence level and at the paragraph level.

Take Note:
Coherence without cohesion has no definite point. It has a weak paragraph development.
For example:
Micah, a friend of mine, will celebrate her birthday tomorrow.
She invited me to come to her house. I cannot attend the celebration because I am not feeling well.

For example:
Micah, a friend of mine, will celebrate her birthday tomorrow. She invited me to come to her house. However, I cannot attend the celebration because I am not feeling well.

Take Note:
Cohesion without coherence leads to illogical presentation of ideas. The writer appears to move on to another related idea without proper transition from the previous idea.

For example:
On top of that, we will also go swimming. My friends and I decided to gather. We are planning to play volleyball.

For example:
My friends and I decided to gather. We are planning to play volleyball. On top of that, we will also go swimming.

Language Use

- refers to the appropriateness of word or vocabulary usage in a text.

Things to Consider in Language Use:
Formal Language

- is used in writing academic, business, and official texts.

Informal Language

- is usually used in writing for oneself or in writing to family, friends, and colleagues.
For Example:
“We asked the secretary to call the professor and get her permission for us to continue our research.”

Unbiased Language

– is the language that is free of racial bias, free of religious bias, and free of gender bias.
For Example:
“A teacher should motivate his students.”

Mechanics

– is the set of convections on how to spell, abbreviate, punctuate, and capitalize.

Convections of Mechanics:
Spelling

– is the forming of words from letters according to accepted usage.
Take Note:
There are two spellings in English, which are American Spelling and British Spelling.

Punctuation Marks

– are symbols used to aid the clarity and comprehension of written language.

Capitalization

– is the act of writing the letters in uppercase. It is an act or process of capitalizing.
